To address these operational voids across all levels, the proponents propose the development of RoadResQ, A Crowd-Service Roadside Rescue Platform in Laguna utilizing Proximity-Based Matching and Symptom-Based Triage. Unlike the standard towing applications that cater only to urban centers and require heavy data, RoadResQ functions as a platform-mediated coordination system specifically engineered for the provincial landscape of Laguna. The platform dynamically connects stranded motorists and vulnerable commuters to a digitally verified network of local responders, ranging from talyers and vulcanizing shops for mechanical repairs, to accredited transport agents for safe rescue rides. To ensure operational precision and security, the system integrates a Symptom-Based Triage Subsystem that automatically dispatches the correctly equipped agent, backed by a strict Identity and Accreditation Subsystem that legally screens all crowdsourced workers. Additionally, it incorporates a Hybrid mobile application and hotline-assisted emergency reporting workflow to bypass internet dead spots and utilizes Service Level Agreement (SLA) timers to enforce rapid response times, ultimately professionalizing and securing the informal roadside assistance sector.


OBJECTIVES OF THE STUDY

General Objective:
The study aims to develop RoadResQ, A Crowd-Service Roadside Rescue Platform in Laguna utilizing Proximity-Based Matching and Symptom-Based Triage.

Specific Objectives:
1.	To develop a Identity and Accreditation Subsystem that requires independent agents ranging from mechanics to legal verification credentials prior to platform activation:
a.	Professional Driver’s License
b.	Vehicle Registration (OR/CR)
c.	Proof of Insurance
d.	NBI Clearance
2.	To design a Hybrid Emergency Reporting Interface integrated with a Symptom-Based Triage subsystem, enabling motorists to submit distress signals via a real-time GPS mobile application or an administrator-assisted hotline.
3.	To implement a Proximity-Based Matching Engine that systematically assigns service requests based on geographic distance, triage classification, and real-time agent availability.
4.	To integrate an SLA and Compliance Subsystem together with a fare computation module.
5.	To construct a Centralized Administrative Dashboard that provides real-time monitoring of dispatch activity, partner availability heatmaps, and automated suspension for low-rated agents.
6.	To evaluate the developed system for its acceptability based on the System Usability Scale (SUS).

CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK

Figure 1 presents the conceptual framework of the RoadResQ system, which illustrates the overall structure and operational flow of the proposed platform. The framework is organized into four primary layers: the Input Layer, Processing Layer, Database Layer, and Output Layer. Additionally, the system relies on an External APIs & Cloud Services module to handle third-party integrations and is accessed through designated platform interfaces (Mobile App and Web Dashboard). These layers describe how data enters the system, how it is processed through various subsystems, how information is stored, and how results are delivered to the system stakeholders. The framework also highlights the interaction between the platform’s primary actors, namely stranded motorists, rescue agents, system administrators, and researchers.
The process begins at the Input Layer, where the system collects the necessary information from its primary users. Stranded motorists initiate service requests through the Emergency Reporting Interface via the Mobile App, which allows users to submit emergency details including automatically captured GPS location, service type selection (repair or transport rescue), symptom classification based on predefined mechanical categories, optional multimedia uploads such as photos or videos of the vehicle condition, and auto-filled user profile information for identification and contact reference. In situations where mobile internet connectivity is unavailable, the system provides Hotline-Assisted Emergency Reporting, where stranded motorists may contact the service hotline and an administrator encodes the emergency details into the system through the Web Dashboard. This feature ensures accessibility for users located in areas with limited network connectivity.
Simultaneously, rescue agents interact with the system through the Agent Registration and Accreditation Interface on the Mobile App. During the registration process, agents submit their required professional credentials through the Credential Submission module, which includes documents such as a Professional Driver’s License, Vehicle Registration (OR/CR), Proof of Insurance, and NBI Clearance. These documents are necessary to verify the legitimacy and legal compliance of participating responders. In addition to credential submission, agents also configure their Operational Profile, which defines their service category (mechanical repair or transport rescue), equipment capabilities (such as battery kits, towing tools, or flatbed vehicles), geographic service coverage area, and their availability status within the platform.
Once the necessary information has been collected, the data proceeds to the Processing Layer, which functions as the central operational component of the system. Within this layer, the Triage Subsystem evaluates and classifies the reported vehicle symptoms to determine the appropriate type of assistance required. Based on this classification, the Proximity-Based Matching Engine supported by the OpenStreetMap (OSM) API for open-source, real-time geographic routing identifies the corresponding rescue agent by evaluating multiple factors such as geographic distance, equipment capability, and real-time availability of registered responders. This process ensures that the system assigns service requests to the designated agent.
In parallel, the Fare Computation Module calculates standardized service costs using predefined rate formulas designed to provide transparent and consistent pricing for emergency services. Meanwhile, the SLA and Compliance Subsystem monitor the service workflow by tracking response times through Service Level Agreement (SLA) timers and implementing breach detection logic to identify delays or violations in the expected service response. Additionally, the Identity and Accreditation Subsystem perform credential validation and manages the approval or rejection workflow for newly registered rescue agents, ensuring that only verified partners are allowed to operate within the platform.
All operational data generated during system activities is stored within the Database Layer, which maintains several system repositories for secure data management. The Identity and Credential Storage repository stores verified documents and accreditation records of registered agents. To optimize performance, heavy multimedia files such as uploaded vehicular damage photos and legal credentials are specifically routed to Firebase Cloud Storage. The Operational State Database tracks real-time operational information such as agent availability status and current deployment activity. Service transactions and monitoring records are stored within the Dispatch and SLA Logs, which maintain a historical record of dispatch operations and SLA performance data. Additionally, the SUS Feedback Repository stores user evaluation responses collected after service completion, which are used for system evaluation purposes.
Finally, the processed information is delivered through the Output Layer, which provides operational results and system interfaces for different stakeholders. Stranded motorists and rescue agents interact with the Live Dispatch and Tracking Interface, which displays real-time service assignment updates, routing information, and calculated service fares. Rescue agents also receive Job and Dispatch Notifications, powered seamlessly by Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM), whenever a service request is assigned to them by the system. At the administrative level, the Centralized Administrative Dashboard provides system administrators (via the Web Dashboard) with real-time monitoring tools for managing dispatch operations, reviewing partner availability, and analyzing SLA performance reports. After service completion, users are invited to participate in a Post-Service SUS Survey, where their feedback is collected and analyzed through the SUS Analytics Dashboard. The results of this evaluation are then utilized by the researchers to assess the system’s perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use based on the System Usability Scale.
